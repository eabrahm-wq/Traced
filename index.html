The 7-Dimension
Rubric

How we trace
1
Ownership & Control
Who holds the equity. Private equity, conglomerate, or founder-led. Strategic conflicts between parent portfolio and brand mission.
2
Ingredient Disclosure
Are individual ingredients and doses fully published? Proprietary blend analysis — what's hidden and what that concealment costs the consumer.
3
Dose Adequacy
Do disclosed quantities match clinical thresholds? Sub-clinical dosing with disclosed ingredients is still a transparency failure.
4
Third-Party Verification
NSF, Informed Sport, USP, B Corp, Fairtrade. Certification vs. marketing claim. Batch-level COAs vs. periodic audits vs. nothing.
5
Label Accuracy
Do front-of-pack claims match what's in the formula? Active lawsuits, FDA warning letters, class action history, ingredient substitution.
6
Marketing vs. Formula
Does what the brand claims to do match what the formula can do? Paid influencer structure, proprietary science trademarking, health halo positioning.
7
Founder Alignment
Is leadership still present? Does the founding mission match current business behavior? Post-acquisition drift tracking.
Green Zone 5+ green dimensions. Ingredient and supply chain transparency is high. May have minor gaps but no active misleading claims and no ownership conflicts that compromise the formula.
Yellow Zone Mixed picture. Typically: good ingredient story with conglomerate ownership, or good ownership with dose opacity. "Watch condition active" means something specific is worth monitoring.
Red Zone 3+ red dimensions or 1 critical failure: active labeling lawsuit, documented formula adulteration, broken supply chain pledges, or systemic proprietary blend concealment.

Patterns
Detected

Cross-category
01
The Conglomerate Acquisition Pattern
A premium health-positioned brand builds an identity around ingredient purity, founder authenticity, or supply chain transparency. A conglomerate acquires it. The formula holds — until it doesn't. The watch condition: whether post-acquisition economics pressure the brand back toward the parent's ingredient standards. Found in every category Traced has reviewed.
Hu → Mondelēz Lily's → Hershey Nuun → Nestlé Liquid IV → Unilever 8Greens → Grove
02
Proprietary Blend Concealment
A formula lists an ingredient (spirulina, ashwagandha, electrolytes) but groups it inside a "proprietary blend" with no individual dose disclosure. This is legal. It is also how you charge $90/month for a product you cannot evaluate. The pattern is heaviest in greens powders but runs across all supplement categories. Traced calculates the average dose per ingredient in every blend it reviews.
AG1 — 49/75 hidden Bloom — 7 blends Organifi 8Greens
03
Mass Balance ≠ Traceability
Third-party certifications from Rainforest Alliance and Fair Trade offer a "mass balance" option: companies purchase a volume of certified supply equivalent to what they use, without requiring the specific product to contain certified ingredients. The certification is real. What it certifies is not what most consumers understand it to certify. This gap is widest in the chocolate category but relevant for any product with sourcing claims.
Nestlé / KitKat Hershey Hu Lily's
04
Trademarked Science
Public or open-source science — WHO oral rehydration formulas, peer-reviewed clinical research — is repackaged behind a trademark to create the appearance of proprietary innovation. The science is real. The trademark implies exclusivity that doesn't exist. Consumers pay a premium for what is, in effect, a branding layer on existing knowledge.
Liquid IV — CTT™ Various greens
05
The Formula Drift Signal
A brand reformulates its core product — adding cheaper emulsifiers, reducing active ingredient concentrations, swapping certified for conventional sources — without disclosing the change to consumers. The first signal is usually found in investor communications, not on the label. CFO cost-reduction language in earnings calls often precedes visible formula changes by 12–18 months.

Why Traced
Exists

The premise

The food and supplement industries have a transparency deficit that regulatory frameworks don't fully close. The FDA does not require pre-market approval for most supplements. Proprietary blends are legal. "Mass balance" certifications are legitimate. Front-of-pack health claims operate in a wide-gray-zone between FDA allowance and consumer understanding. None of this is news to the industry. Most of it is news to the average buyer.

Traced is not a watchdog or an advocacy organization. It is a research library. The goal is to put the relevant information — ownership structure, formula analysis, third-party verification status, marketing claim accuracy — in one place, organized clearly enough that the first-time visitor can orient themselves without spending an afternoon reading SEC filings.

The 7-dimension rubric is designed to be replicable and auditable. Every score is documented. Every finding cites a source. When the research is uncertain or incomplete, that uncertainty is disclosed. The standard we're holding brands to is the standard they've set for themselves in their own marketing.

◎ Editorial, not algorithmic. Every profile is written and scored by a human researcher. No automated scoring. No affiliate links. No brand partnerships.
◎ Sources cited. Every factual claim in every profile links to a primary source — company filings, peer-reviewed literature, regulatory documents, court filings, or the brand's own published label.
◎ Scores can change. Formulas change. Ownership changes. Lawsuits resolve. Certifications lapse. The profiles are reviewed when there is a material change to any dimension.
◎ Green is achievable. The rubric is not designed to make everyone fail. Ritual, DripDrop, Tony's Chocolonely, and others have built genuine transparency into their operations. The benchmark exists to be replicated.
◎ The goal is orientation. Not alarm. Not education. If you leave a Traced profile knowing one specific, accurate thing you didn't know before — that's the win.
